Urgency is a powerful weapon. If someone says something has to happen now, people typically leap right into action and only reflect afterwards. When the Dutch cabinet presents urgent measures, the House of Representatives of the Dutch parliament enters urgent consultations, so that the urgent measures can be rolled out with urgency, via an urgency procedure. Within a few weeks -- or sometimes even a few days, regulatory bodies and NGOs issue urgent responses. But the time needed to process this feedback is, of course, not available. Urgency rarely leads to strong legislation, let alone legislation that can count on broad-based support.

Tech companies also like to invoke urgency. On February 19, 2023, Sam Altman, CEO of the company behind ChatGPT, tweeted: 'the adaptation to a world deeply integrated with AI tools is probably going to happen pretty quickly; the benefits (and fun!) have too much upside.' He concluded his tweet with the words: 'having time to understand what's happening, how people want to use these tools, and how society can co-evolve is critical.'

The idea that society has to adjust to technological advancement is not new, and Altman's tweet was soon discredited by many as an outstanding example of tone-deaf technological determinism, i.e., the belief that technology follows its own set of laws and is a force outside our control, with its own will and timeline, and that sooner or later society will have to adapt to it.

Obviously, it is not technology itself, but the makers of technology that are hurtling forward. In the case in question, Altman himself and his company, OpenAI. Million- and billion-dollar companies such as his see opportunities for making high profits in the short term by taking advantage of missing 'foolproof' laws and legislation. And by exploiting vulnerabilities in our democratic and rule-based processes. They're able to act more quickly than anyone else because they're not encumbered by democratic decision making processes, responsibility for a collective and just future or any concern for the victims of their reckless actions.

Take facial-recognition technology. To learn about the history of facial recognition scandals, all you need to do is follow the quest of its creators for 'product-market fit.' One of the earliest examples of the large-scale deployment of facial-recognition technology was VK, the Russian service that helped users find the social media profiles of women they had photographed on the street. When this 'service' ultimately collapsed, something similar popped up elsewhere: Clearview AI. Clearview specialized in tracing. The idea is simple: a police officer takes a picture of someone, uploads it to Clearview and gets a range of online sources back, with photos of the person.

It's patently obvious that what Clearview AI does is totally unlawful. Several European privacy oversight bodies have reprimanded the company for a variety of reasons. The French regulator called on it several times to halt its activities. Leaked sources also appeared to show that the Dutch police were using Clearview AI; the responsible government minister would neither confirm nor deny this.

In the meantime, the police have published their Framework for the deployment of facial-recognition technology. In the framework's half-page-long preface, fast and related words are already used four times. Further on, the authors write: 'Developments of both a societal and technological nature are occurring in rapid succession, often in a tempo that makes it difficult to keep pace with with formal legislation.' The conclusion drawn by the police is that they are forced to deploy this technology -- outside the law. Urgency on the part of the vendor to market its discriminatory and potentially highly repressive products translates into urgency on the part of the potential purchaser to do 'something' with them. Citizens lose important protections and at the same time are confronted with an unpredictable and unreliable government.

From the standpoint of the police, facial-recognition technology is clearly an available alternative. However, as we see it, it's the wrong one. Rather than viewing the sluggishness and many-voiced character of our democratic and rules-based processes as an obstacle, it is, we dare say, of the highest urgency for the government to start regarding these as a source of power. The question should not be, how can society can keep pace with technological developments?, but rather, how can the developers of technology best accommodate us?

As we see it, 2023 will be dominated by the themes, AI legislation, the new rights of platform users and limiting government surveillance and censorship. As an organization, we invest in technological research, European public communication and broad-based opposition to harmful digitization. But before speeding ahead, we'd like to take a moment to look back on the successes of the past year. Interested?

Discussion

This year, we had four unexpected windfalls. We received support from Stichting Sub3, as well as a large one-time gift from a company, and the police were (again) ordered to pay us a fifteen-thousand-euro penalty. Our umbrella organization, European Digital Rights, twice awarded us a small grant of ca. € 10,000 to support our European activities.

Our desired donor growth of ca. € 30,000 remained entirely unrealized. Although we succeeded in finding many new donors, an above-average number also departed. The increased costs of living and energy, inflation: some donors terminated their standing donation with pain in their hearts or found it necessary to reduce their contribution. Although, thanks to the above-mentioned windfalls, this won't cause problems in the short term, it is a worrying development for the longer term, one which could lead to forced down-sizing.

On the expenditures side, four things stood out. After a number of years with fewer staff than anticipated, and, as a result, lower costs than budgeted for, we've finally cleared our backlog. At the same time, we've been incrementally increasing our expenditures in the area of our own communication, in order to improve awareness of our organization and its goals among a broad, interested public. Aside from this, generally speaking, expenditures were somewhat lower, and it was luckily not necessary to fall back on our long-term illness reserve!

All in all, this means that we close the year with a modest positive result which will be put to use in the coming years. It brings our special-purpose reserve to ca. € 600,000 and our continuity reserve to roughly 30% of our expected costs for the coming year.

In 2022, 7.1% of funds spent went to fundraising and 5.1% to administration. 85.9% was spent directly on achieving our objectives. This is in line with our principle of not spending more than 20% of our funds and our time on fundraising and administration.

Our annual financial report for 2022 was audited by WITh Accountants and can be downloaded from: https://2022.bitsoffreedom.nl/.

Foundations

For a portion of our work, it's difficult to obtain (sufficient) financial support from individuals. For this reason, foundations are extremely important to us, both for the sake of the sustainability of our work and the further development and growth of our organization. In some cases, foundations make it possible, at short notice, to take important steps at crucial moments, e.g., by financing a new role or research into a new work program.

In 2022, we received support for our core costs from:

  • The Adessium Foundation: works toward a society in which people live in harmony with one another and with their surroundings.
  • The Ford Foundation: works toward a fair and just society.
  • The Limelight Foundation: works toward a robust and free information ecosystem.
  • Open Society Foundations (OSF): work toward robust and inclusive democracies.
  • Stichting Internet Domeinregistratie Nederland (SIDN): works toward a carefree digital world with opportunities for all.
  • Stichting Democratie & Media: invests in independent, critical media and is dedicated to a robust democracy, based on the rule of law.
  • Stichting Sub3: through the promotion of equality, Sub3 strives to provide people and organizations with opportunities for further development.

We received project financing from Aspiration Tech for our public-interest tech pilot, from Goeie Grutten for Data. Zo zit dat., from Luminate for Litigating Big Tech (in cooperation with PILP), from Gieskes-Strijbis Fonds for our work for a just society and for our partnership with Amnesty International, the Open State Foundation and Waag, and from Open Society Foundations within the context of the New Executives Fund.

Donors

We concluded 2022 with 3,167 steady donors, from whom we receive funds on a monthly, quarterly or yearly basis. We also received donations from individuals 1,190 times via our website or by transfer. Donations from individual donors totaled € 368,901.

A strong and stabile support base is indispensable to Bits of Freedom. Our donors not only enable us to work independently, but also play an essential role in amplifying our message and stimulating support for our organization. In 2022, we strengthened our connection to our support base by, among other things, expanding our quadrennial donor update, holding special donation events and involving donors more with our work. We also met up with many donors during an exclusive event at IMPAKT, Utrecht.

Something else we focused on in 2022 was tax-advantageous donation. In this connection, we simplified the registration process by means of a special page on our website, to which we called attention during our Ready-made Resolutions year-end campaign. With success! Many steady donors re-registered as ANBI (public benefit organization) donors. On the one hand, this is financially advantageous to them and, on the other, it gives us the security of being able to count on their support for five years. Curious? You can read all about it on our website.

In our expenditures, we allow for a staffing of 11.5 FTEs. A number of specific projects and events have already been budgeted at ca. € 340,000, with BOF's own share set at € 25,000. Most of the revenues have already been promised.

With six foundations, we have multi-year arrangements for financial support. Adessium Foundation is supporting us through 2025 with € 150,000 per year, Stichting Democratie & Media is supporting us through 2024 with € 35,000 per year and from Open Society Foundations (OSF) we received, in 2021, € 231,000 for the period, 2022 through 2024. Limelight Foundation is supporting us through 2024 with € 85,000 per year, Stichting Internet Domeinregistratie Nederland (SIDN) is supporting us up to 2024 with € 30,000 per year and, finally, Sub3 is supporting us for the period from April, 2022 to April, 2024 with an amount totaling € 400,000. We hope to raise an additional € 175,000 in foundation support in 2023.

We hope to raise a total of € 424,000 from donors in 2023, which represents a substantial increase of ca. € 55,000 as compared to the past year. We hope to raise € 109,000 from companies.

Should we realize the above ambitions, the difference between estimated revenues and expenditures will come to - € 204,987. This negative result will be supplemented with funds from the special-purpose reserve. Our continuity reserve will not be tapped in 2023.

Our financing in the coming years

Desired financing mix

Bits of Freedom assigns great importance to having a sustainable financial situation, and it is our intention to remain fully independent. In drawing up our financial planning, we always try to look five years ahead. Our wish is for half of our revenues to come from individual donors. We would like to receive ca. 35% of our core revenues from funds and foundations and hope eventually to be able to receive ca. 15% from companies. At the present time, the share in revenues from donors (27% in 2023) and from company sponsoring (7% in 2023) is too low as compared to revenues from foundations (47% in 2023). Finally, we see that the share in project financing (19% in 2023) is increasing each year. This involves risks in the areas of effectivity, knowledge and expertise security and good employment practice.

Challenges

In 2023, we expect to realize our ambitions with regard to donor revenue by focusing on structural arrangements and by reaching and attracting both existing and new supporters, facilitated by an extensive revamp of our own donation software and an enlargement of our shop, as well as through campaigns. E.g., in consultation with donors, we are currently developing a new donor flow that will make donating to Bits of Freedom even simpler and more rewarding than it already is. Further, there will be a greater focus on micro-donations, which are also to be given a permanent location within our channels. Finally, we can announce that our donor environment has been expanded to include new functions which simplify tax-advantageous donation and give donors greater control over their donation and communication preferences. We are proud of being able to develop this within the context of our current self-developed, open-source donation software that gives top-priority to privacy.

We also hope to receive European financing for our growing European ambitions in the areas of public campaigns and enforcement of the Digital Services Act and Digital Markets Act. In part for this reason, we have decided to give greater attention to our English-language communication and international visibility.

In the areas of asset management and financial reserves, we are working towards maintaining a continuity reserve of minimally 25% of our annual costs for the coming year. Such a percentage is normal for NGOs of our size. We expect to have a continuity reserve of ca. 30% at the end of 2023.

Reach

In 2022, we reached millions of people via our own newsletters, social media channels and website. We also stimulated new interest through more than a hundred media appearances. We realized several micro-campaigns to further increase awareness about our activities and spoke at universities and on panels. We also wrote several blogs to keep our support base apprised of developments. Finally, we carried out more than a hundred lobby meetings and spoke three times as experts in the House of Representatives and once in the Senate.
